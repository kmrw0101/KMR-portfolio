# **The Destruction of the Rondo Neighborhood – Geocoding Historical Architecture (2011)**

This project documents the **loss of the Rondo neighborhood in St. Paul, Minnesota**, a once‑thriving, culturally rich community destroyed during the construction of **Interstate 94** in the late 1950s and 1960s.  
The work combines **historical geocoding**, **digitized archival mapping**, and **spatial analysis** to illustrate how federal highway policy reshaped the urban landscape and displaced a racially diverse population.

This project is part of my **GIS portfolio archive**, highlighting early my work in historical mapping and spatial analysis.

## 🧩 How the Project Was Built (2011)

This project reconstructs the Rondo neighborhood using a combination of **historical cartography**, **geocoding**, and **digitization**:

### **✔ Geocoded historical architecture**
- Mapped buildings, institutions, and culturally significant locations destroyed during I‑94 construction.  
- Used archival addresses and historical records to place features accurately.

### **✔ Digitized archival Rondo Avenue map**
- Scanned and digitized an early 20th‑century map of the neighborhood.  
- Overlaid the historic street grid with the modern freeway alignment to show contrast.

### **✔ Spatial analysis of displacement**
- Identified patterns of architectural loss.  
- Highlighted the cultural and demographic diversity of the neighborhood prior to demolition.

### **✔ Historical context**
- Grounded in the Federal‑Aid Highway Act of 1956, which authorized 41,000 miles of new interstate highways.  
- Demonstrates how “progress” often came at the expense of established communities.

## 🗺️ About the Visualization

The Rondo project illustrates:

- The architectural and cultural richness of the neighborhood  
- The locations of historically significant buildings  
- The alignment of Interstate 94 through the heart of the community  
- The contrast between the 1960s neighborhood and the modern freeway corridor  
- The human and spatial impact of mid‑century urban renewal  

This work represents my graduate studies dive into **historical GIS**, combining archival research, geocoding, and map digitization to tell the story of a community erased by infrastructure.
